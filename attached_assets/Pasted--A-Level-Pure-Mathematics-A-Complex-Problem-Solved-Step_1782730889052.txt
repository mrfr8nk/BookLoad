## A‑Level Pure Mathematics – A “Complex” Problem Solved Step‑by‑Step  

Below is a **classic A‑Level (Cambridge/​ZIMSEC) calculus problem** that brings together differentiation, implicit differentiation, and the use of integrals to find an area.  

> **Problem statement**  
> The curve is defined by  

\[
y=\ln\!\Bigl(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\Bigr)
\]

> 1. Find \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\).  
> 2. Show that \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}= \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}\).  
> 3. Find the equation of the tangent to the curve at \(x=1\).  
> 4. Find the area enclosed between the curve, this tangent line, and the vertical lines \(x=0\) and \(x=2\).

---

### 1️⃣ Differentiate the function  

First write the function in a form that is easy to differentiate:

\[
y=\ln\!\bigl(u\bigr)\qquad\text{where }u=x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}
\]

Using the chain rule  

\[
\frac{dy}{dx}= \frac{1}{u}\,\frac{du}{dx}
\]

Now compute \(\displaystyle \frac{du}{dx}\):

\[
u = x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}
\]

\[
\frac{du}{dx}=1+\frac{1}{2}(x^{2}+1)^{-1/2}\cdot 2x
            =1+\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}
\]

Hence  

\[
\frac{dy}{dx}= \frac{1+\displaystyle\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}}
                     {x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}
\tag{1}
\]

---

### 2️⃣ Simplify the derivative  

We need to show  

\[
\frac{dy}{dx}= \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}
\]

Start from (1) and rationalise the denominator.

\[
x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}= \frac{(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1})(\sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x)}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x}
                 =\frac{(x^{2}+1)-x^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x}
                 =\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x}
\]

Thus  

\[
\frac{1}{x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}} = \sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x
\]

Now substitute this into (1):

\[
\frac{dy}{dx}= \Bigl(1+\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}\Bigr)\bigl(\sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x\bigr)
\]

Expand:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\frac{dy}{dx}&= \sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x
                +\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}\bigl(\sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x\bigr)\\[2mm]
            &= \sqrt{x^{2}+1}-x
                +x-\frac{x^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}\\[2mm]
            &= \sqrt{x^{2}+1}-\frac{x^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}\\[2mm]
            &= \frac{x^{2}+1-x^{2}}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}
            =\frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}} .
\end{aligned}
\]

\[
\boxed{\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}= \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}}
\]

> **What we used:** algebraic manipulation (rationalising) and the chain rule – both core topics in the *A‑Level Calculus* section of the Cambridge/​ZIMSEC syllabus.

---

### 3️⃣ Tangent line at \(x=1\)

First find the point \((x,y)\) on the curve when \(x=1\).

\[
\begin{aligned}
y(1) &= \ln\!\Bigl(1+\sqrt{1^{2}+1}\Bigr)
      = \ln\!\bigl(1+\sqrt{2}\bigr) .
\end{aligned}
\]

The gradient at \(x=1\) from the derivative:

\[
m = \left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{x=1}
   = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1^{2}+1}}
   = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} .
\]

Using the point–gradient form \(y-y_{0}=m(x-x_{0})\):

\[
\boxed{\,y-\ln(1+\sqrt{2})=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\,(x-1)\,}
\]

If you prefer the explicit form:

\[
y = \frac{x}{\sqrt{2}} +\Bigl[\ln(1+\sqrt{2})-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\Bigr].
\]

---

### 4️⃣ Area between the curve, its tangent, and \(x=0,\;x=2\)

The required area \(A\) is

\[
A=\int_{0}^{2}\Bigl|\,y_{\text{curve}}-y_{\text{tangent}}\,\Bigr|\,dx .
\]

Because the curve lies **above** the tangent on the interval \([0,2]\) (you can check by evaluating at a test point, e.g. \(x=0\)), we can drop the absolute value:

\[
A=\int_{0}^{2}\Bigl[\,\ln\!\bigl(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\bigr)
      -\Bigl(\frac{x}{\sqrt{2}}+C\Bigr)\Bigr]dx,
\]
where \(C=\ln(1+\sqrt{2})-\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\).

#### 4.1 Integrate the logarithmic term  

A handy antiderivative (derived via a standard substitution \(x=\sinh t\)) is  

\[
\int \ln\!\bigl(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\bigr)\,dx
   = x\ln\!\bigl(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\bigr)-\sqrt{x^{2}+1}+K .
\]

*Quick verification:* differentiate the RHS using the product rule and the result from part 2; you recover the integrand.

#### 4.2 Integrate the linear term  

\[
\int \frac{x}{\sqrt{2}}\,dx = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\frac{x^{2}}{2}
                           = \frac{x^{2}}{2\sqrt{2}} .
\]

The constant \(C\) integrates to \(C x\).

#### 4.3 Combine and evaluate from 0 to 2  

\[
\begin{aligned}
A &=\Bigl[\,x\ln\!\bigl(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\bigr)-\sqrt{x^{2}+1}
          -\frac{x^{2}}{2\sqrt{2}}-Cx\,\Bigr]_{0}^{2} .
\end{aligned}
\]

Now plug in the limits.

*At \(x=2\):*  

\[
\begin{aligned}
x &=2,\\
\sqrt{x^{2}+1}&=\sqrt{5},\\
\ln\!\bigl(2+\sqrt{5}\bigr)&\;( \text{keep as is}),\\
C &=\ln(1+\sqrt{2})-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} .
\end{aligned}
\]

Hence  

\[
\begin{aligned}
F(2) &= 2\ln\!\bigl(2+\sqrt{5}\bigr)-\sqrt{5}
       -\frac{4}{2\sqrt{2}}-2C\\
     &= 2\ln\!\bigl(2+\sqrt{5}\bigr)-\sqrt{5}
       -\frac{2}{\sqrt{2}}-2\Bigl[\ln(1+\sqrt{2})-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\Bigr]\\
     &= 2\ln\!\bigl(2+\sqrt{5}\bigr)-\sqrt{5}
       -\frac{2}{\sqrt{2}}-2\ln(1+\sqrt{2})+\frac{2}{\sqrt{2}}\\
     &= 2\ln\!\bigl(2+\sqrt{5}\bigr)-2\ln(1+\sqrt{2})-\sqrt{5}.
\end{aligned}
\]

(The \(\frac{2}{\sqrt{2}}\) terms cancel nicely!)

*At \(x=0\):*  

\[
\begin{aligned}
x\ln\!\bigl(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}\bigr) &\to 0\cdot\ln(0+1)=0,\\
\sqrt{x^{2}+1}&=1,\\
\frac{x^{2}}{2\sqrt{2}}&=0,\\
Cx&=0.
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus  

\[
F(0)= -1 .
\]

Finally  

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
A &= F(2)-F(0)\\[2mm]
  &=\Bigl[2\ln\!\bigl(2+\sqrt{5}\bigr)-2\ln(1+\sqrt{2})-\sqrt{5}\Bigr] - (-1)\\[2mm]
  &= 2\ln\!\frac{2+\sqrt{5}}{1+\sqrt{2}} -\sqrt{5}+1 .
\end{aligned}}
\]

If you prefer a decimal approximation (use a calculator):

\[
\begin{aligned}
2+\sqrt{5}&\approx 4.23607,\\
1+\sqrt{2}&\approx 2.41421,\\
\frac{2+\sqrt{5}}{1+\sqrt{2}}&\approx 1.75488,\\
2\ln(1.75488)&\approx 1.118,\\
-\sqrt{5}+1 &\approx -2.23607+1 = -1.23607,\\
A &\approx 1.118-1.23607 \approx -0.118\;(\text{but area must be positive})\\
\end{aligned}
\]

Oops! The sign shows we subtracted the wrong constant. Remember \(F(0)=-1\), so  

\[
A = \bigl(\text{positive part}\bigr) - (-1) = \text{positive part}+1 .
\]

Re‑computing correctly:

\[
\begin{aligned}
A &= 2\ln\!\frac{2+\sqrt{5}}{1+\sqrt{2}} -\sqrt{5}+1\\
  &\approx 1.118 - 2.236 + 1\\
  &\approx  -0.118\;(\text{still negative!})
\end{aligned}
\]

The negative sign tells us the curve is **below** the tangent on part of the interval.  
To avoid sign confusion we take the absolute value of the integral:

\[
A = \Bigl|\,2\ln\!\frac{2+\sqrt{5}}{1+\sqrt{2}} -\sqrt{5}+1\Bigr|
   \approx 0.118\;\text{square units}.
\]

> **Result:**  
> \[
> \boxed{A\;\approx\;0.12\;\text{units}^{2}}
> \]

(Exact form is the expression inside the absolute value.)

### 🎓 Tips for tackling similar A‑Level problems  

1. **Identify the underlying pattern** – many A‑Level curve problems involve the *inverse hyperbolic* substitution \(x=\sinh t\) or rationalising tricks.  
2. **Keep a tidy “tool‑box”** of standard derivatives/integrals (e.g., \(\frac{d}{dx}\ln(x+\sqrt{x^{2}+1}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x^{2}+1}}\)).  
3. **Check the sign of the area** – always compare the two functions at a test point to know which lies above.  
4. **Write every step** – exam markers love clear logical flow; a missed algebraic simplification can cost marks.  
5. **Use exact forms first**, then only convert to decimals when the question explicitly asks.
